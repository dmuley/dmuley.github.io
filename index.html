Intro

I am a senior undergrad double-majoring in Physics and Astrophysics at UC Berkeley. Since starting college I have pursued various research projects in astronomy (see my CV), but my first major work was a 2019 hydrodynamical study of the PDS 70 disk-planet system with Jeffrey Fung, a NASA Sagan postdoctoral fellow at Berkeley. Our simple model accurately reproduced the disk's observed structure, and encouraged my interest in the intersection of theory and observations.

Besides research, I have been a teaching assistant for Alex Filippenko's Astronomy C10 general-astronomy class at Berkeley, leading weekly discussion sections. The course materials I composed can be found here. I have also taught for the Be a Scientist program, helping middle-school students devise and run science experiments. In my spare time, I enjoy hiking on local trails and trying new places to eat.

Research

Metallicity of dwarf galaxies

Advisor: Coral Wheeler (NASA Hubble Fellow), Philip F. Hopkins (Professor of Theoretical Astrophysics, Caltech)

The FIRE project has, over the past decade, produced increasingly accurate simulations of galaxy evolution, addressing problems from missing-satellites to the Kennicutt-Schmidt relation. Among the remaining discrepancies with observations is the fact that metallicities of simulated dwarf galaxies are far lower than those observed, and that elemental abundance ratios at the lowest metallicities lack dispersion. A possible reason is that elemental yields in supernovae are averaged over the IMF—acceptable for the aggregate properties in Milky Way-like systems with dynamical times far exceeding massive-star lifetimes, but less so in dwarf galaxies.

To remedy this problem, I spent my Summer 2019 as a Caltech SURF Fellow, implementing time-dependent yields from the NuGrid stellar-evolution suite into FIRE. Currently we are running simulations to measure the impact that this may have on dwarf galaxies, and preparing an initial paper. Future directions may include using the improved abundance ratios to perform mock galactic archaeology on stellar populations in simulated Milky Way-type galaxies.

Protoplanetary disks

Fung, Jeffrey; Muley, Dhruv. "A staggered semi-analytic method for simulating dust grains subject to gas drag," The Astrophysical Journal Supplement Series, 244, 2. (2019; ArXiv: 1909.02006)
Muley, Dhruv; Fung, Jeffrey; van der Marel, Nienke. "PDS 70: A transition disk sculpted by a single planet," The Astrophysical Journal Letters, 879, 1. (2019; ArXiv: 1902.07191)
Advisor: Jeffrey Fung, NASA Sagan Fellow

Planets have long been believed to have formed in circumstellar disks, but how exactly they influence disk evolution and dispersal has remained a mystery. The PDS 70 system—one of a handful in which a planet candidate has been directly imaged within its natal disk—provides a valuable window onto this process. Traditional theory states that the super-Jupiter companion, located at 22 au, should exchange angular momentum with disk material and carve out a gap of approximately equal width. Indeed, PDS 70 does have a gap—but it extends to ~65 AU, making it twice as wide as the theory predicts! To make matters more puzzling, this wide gap is not an anomaly, but rather represents many late-stage "transition disks" in the process of dispersing to give way to planetary systems.

We investigated this conundrum using the PEnGUIn hydrodynamical code, simulating the accretion and orbital evolution of the companion, PDS 70b, in its disk over the system's ~5 Myr lifetime. We find that when the planet reaches super-Jupiter mass, it spontaneously becomes eccentric and accretes from a broader region of the disk. This "fiducial" model accurately reproduces the gap profile observed in PDS 70, which our 100+ alternative models (varying disk temperature, density, fixed vs. moving planets, etc.) failed to do. Moreover, the fiducial setup is simple and requires little fine-tuning for its predictive power. Our results show that single, super-Jupiter planets can sculpt the characteristic wide cavities of transition disks, and that the high eccentricities measured for such planets may in part arise from disk-planet interaction.



Simulating PDS 70's wide gap—in particular, its observational signature in dust continuum—raised our interest in the dynamics of dust in protoplanetary disks. To this end, we devised a new integration scheme for dust under gas drag---the "staggered semi-analytic" (SSA) method. In various test problems, SSA displays greater accuracy and stability than prevailing dust-integration methods, sometimes by orders of magnitude.
