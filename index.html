Intro

I am a junior double-majoring in Physics and Astrophysics at UC Berkeley. Since starting undergrad I have pursued various side projects in astronomy (see CV), but my first serious work was a 2019 hydrodynamical study of the PDS 70 disk-planet system with Jeffrey Fung, a NASA Sagan Fellow at Berkeley. Our simple model accurately reproduced the disk's observed structure, and encouraged my interest in the intersection of theory and observations.

Besides research, I have been a teaching assistant for Alex Filippenko's Astronomy C10 general-astronomy class at Berkeley, leading weekly discussion sections. The course materials I composed can be found here. I have also taught for the Be a Scientist program, helping middle-school students devise and run science experiments. In my spare time, I enjoy hiking on local trails and trying new places to eat.

Research

PDS 70

Planets have long been believed to have formed in circumstellar disks, but how exactly planets influence disk evolution and dispersal has remained a mystery. The PDS 70 system—one of a handful in which a planet candidate has been directly imaged within its natal disk—provides a valuable window onto this process. Traditional theory predicts that the planet, located at 22 au, should carve out a gap with an outer ring at ~44 AU by exchanging angular momentum with the disk. Indeed, PDS 70 does have a gap—but it extends to ~65 AU, making it twice as wide as it should be! To make matters worse, the gap size of PDS 70 is not isolated, but rather representative of a large population of "transition disks" thought to represent late-stage disk evolution.

We investigated this question using the PEnGUIn hydrodynamical code, simulating the accretion and orbital evolution of PDS 70b over the ~5 Myr age of the system. When the planet reaches roughly 2.5 Jupiter masses, it spontaneously becomes eccentric by its gravitational interaction with the disk; this gives the planet access to wider region of the disk, which it then consumes by accretion. This "fiducial" model accurately reproduces the gap profile observed in PDS 70, which our 100+ alternative models (varying disk temperature, density, fixed vs. moving planets, etc.) failed to do; moreover, it is simple, requiring little fine-tuning for its predictive power. Our results show that single, super-Jupiter planets can sculpt the characteristic wide cavities of transition disks, and that the high eccentricities measured for such planets may in part arise from disk-planet interaction.
